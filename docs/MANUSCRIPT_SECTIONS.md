## 2.9 Method 9: Multi attribute yield curve matching

We implemented the multi attribute curve matching approach of Tompalski et al. (2016) with the operational refinements of Tompalski et al. (2018). A curve library was constructed by running AcadianGY v12.3.6 (Weiskittel et al. 2016) forward for 100 years from nine representative starting tree lists, one for each combination of three productivity strata (Low, Medium, High) and three species compositions (coniferous, mixed, deciduous). Starting tree lists were built by pooling Howland plot measurements within each stratum by composition cell, with expansion factors divided by the number of pooled plots so each template represents the mean per hectare condition rather than a summed density. AcadianGY was run with INGROWTH = Y, MinDBH = 2.54 cm, CutPoint = 0.95, climate site index 12, elevation 60 m. The resulting library of 909 (template, year offset) observations covers the AGB trajectory space from 2015 out to a 100 year horizon.

For each 30 m pixel, the four candidate (template, year offset) combinations with the smallest absolute difference from observed 2015 AGB were selected, one per template. The four candidate AGB values at each target horizon (2025, 2035, 2075) were combined as a weighted mean using the ABA attribute R² values of the 2015 Howland cross validation (AGB R² = 0.71, DBH R² = 0.65, BA R² = 0.79, top height R² = 0.82), following Tompalski et al. (2016) Equation 1. Per pixel uncertainty was the relative spread among candidates (Tompalski et al. 2016 Equation 2). Validation at 2025 used the independent 2025 MELiTE ABA AGB raster as the reference.

## 2.10 Method 10: Wall to wall directional 3D projection (pure Kohek)

We implemented the directional projection method of Kohek et al. (2022) wall to wall at Howland, substituting AcadianGY v12.3.6 (Weiskittel et al. 2016) for BWINPro to supply species appropriate growth rates for the eastern spruce fir and northern hardwood forest types. To make wall to wall execution tractable at the ~13 GB 92 pts m⁻² site scale, the 2025 normalized ALS was decimated to 25 pts m⁻² by random thinning before segmentation. Individual tree crowns were segmented from the decimated ALS using the Li et al. (2012) algorithm in lidR (Roussel et al. 2020) with dt1 = 1.5 m, dt2 = 2.0 m, R = 2.0 m, and minimum tree height 3 m. Segmentation was distributed across the 25 tiles in parallel using the future R package (Bengtsson 2021).

Each segmented tree was assigned a species composition via nearest neighbor lookup from the 2015 field plots. DBH was imputed from segmented apex height via a default power H D form. Per tree AGB was computed via the Chojnacky et al. (2014) generalized equation. Crown hulls were expanded asymmetrically using Kohek et al. (2022) Equations 7 through 9 with the published baseline coefficient M₀ = 0.2 and shading coefficient M₁ = 0.9, modulated by a local shading proxy from 5 m neighborhood maximum canopy height. Validation used an 80 to 20 pixel level train to test split: the linear calibration was fit on the training fraction and evaluated on the held out test fraction, yielding an honest out of sample RMSE estimate.

## 2.11 Method 11: Hybrid A directional projection (ITC anchor, default Kohek)

Method 11 substitutes the 2017 ALS ITC geopackage (208,632 wall to wall trees with per tree height and crown radius) as the starting state, avoiding fresh 2025 point cloud segmentation. Each 2017 ITC tree was assigned a species composition from the nearest field plot and a productivity stratum (Low, Medium, High) from the observed 2015 AGB tercile of its containing 10 m pixel. DBH was imputed from 2017 height via a default power H D form, and per tree height was projected forward to 2025, 2035, 2045, and 2075 by scaling by the top height ratio of the corresponding stratum by composition trajectory from the Method 9 curve library. Per horizon DBH was derived from projected height; per tree AGB via Chojnacky et al. (2014). The Kohek asymmetric expansion used published defaults M₀ = 0.2 and M₁ = 0.9.

The pixel level calibration that converts raw ITC derived AGB to predicted AGB was fit **separately for each of the nine stratum by composition combinations** rather than as a single global transform. This stratified calibration restores per pixel discrimination that a single global alpha and beta would destroy by shrinking every prediction toward the site mean. Validation at 2025 used the independent 2025 MELiTE ABA AGB raster.

## 2.12 Method 12: Hybrid B directional projection (ITC + TLS calibration)

Method 12 is identical to Method 11 except that (a) the H D allometry is fit from the 2025 TLS tree list rather than taken from literature, and (b) the Kohek M₀ and M₁ coefficients are fit from paired (2017 ALS crown radius, 2025 TLS inferred crown radius) observations within the TLS footprint. TLS trees with reported DBH and location confidence ≥ 50 (n = 3,510) were fit to DBH = a × Ht^b via nonlinear least squares (a = 4.12, b = 0.586). Paired trees within a 3 m proximity threshold (n = 504) supplied growth ratio observations, regressed against the local shading proxy to give M₀ ≈ 0 and M₁ = 0.40. The pixel level calibration was stratified identically to Method 11.

## Discussion additions

The four new methods add complementary positions to the accuracy, cost, and coverage landscape in Table 4. Methods 10, 11, and 12 cluster tightly in the 24.7 to 26.4% RMSE range at $2.65 to $8.20 per acre, all of them wall to wall and all demonstrably out of sample honest. Method 11 (hybrid A) achieves the lowest RMSE at 24.7% (95% CI 24.5 to 25.0) at the lowest cost of the three at $2.65 per acre, and its R² of 0.20 (up from 0.07 with a single pooled calibration) confirms that the method can discriminate across pixels once the calibration is stratified by local composition and productivity. Method 10 (pure Kohek on decimated ALS) matches Method 11's discrimination (R² = 0.25) but at three times the cost because it re-segments the 2025 point cloud from scratch. Method 12 (hybrid B) is slightly worse than Method 11 because the TLS derived H D allometry saturates more steeply than literature forms and the Kohek coefficients fit on only 504 paired observations were noisier than the published defaults; this is a useful negative result about the limits of naive local calibration with modest n.

None of the four new methods beats ITC + FVS ACD (10% RMSE at $2.18 per acre) or the targeted methods (TLS + projection at 7%, Field inventory at 8%). The directional projection family therefore does not dominate the best value quadrant at Howland; it competes with area based LiDAR (15% at $0.91) on cost but sits well behind on accuracy. The value of the directional 3D projection approach is instead in the individual tree resolution of its outputs (useful for silvicultural and carbon accounting applications that need per tree attribution) rather than pixel scale AGB accuracy.

Method 9 (curve matching) remains the most operationally portable choice at $1.50 per acre because it requires no per site ITC segmentation, only an ABA derived AGB and a site agnostic growth library. Its 35.9% RMSE reflects the intrinsic penalty of snapping each pixel to one of nine template trajectories without accounting for within stratum variation. At long horizons (50 year) it performs comparably to the other library based and hybrid methods, suggesting the information gain from full per tree bookkeeping degrades with projection length as individual tree mortality becomes the dominant source of uncertainty.
